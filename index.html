Exploring Spectral Losses, Fourier NCAs, and LTI Dynamics

Welcome everyone. Today we are looking at self-organizing systems, specifically Neural Cellular Automata (NCA), but from a different angle: through the lens of the frequency and spectral domains. We will explore how alternative representations alter coordinate systems, simplify operations, and provide global coordination.

Why the Spectral Domain in ALife?

Inherent Limitations of Spatial NCAs

  • Localized Receptive Fields: Message passing is bounded, requiring many steps to coordinate distal regions.
  • Boundary Artifacts: Standard grid representations struggle at borders without arbitrary padding rules.
  • Resolution Dependence: Scales poorly when migrating trained weights to higher-resolution canvases.

The Fourier Advantage

  • Global Communication: A single coefficient in FFT space influences the entire spatial image natively.
  • Periodic Boundaries: Frequency domain representations naturally provide periodic (toroidal) boundary conditions.
  • Closed-Form Tractability: Linear Time-Invariant (LTI) systems can be evaluated directly across multiple steps.
In typical spatial NCAs, coordination across distances is slow because information must diffuse step-by-step. In the frequency domain, every coefficient has a global receptive field. This provides resolution-independence and natural boundary conditions without manual padding.

Running the NCA inside the FFT

Spectral State Space

  • Instead of updating pixel states, we execute cellular updates directly in the Fourier spectrum.
  • For real-valued spatial output, we only need to update half of the spectrum (exploiting conjugate symmetry).
  • Convolutions inside spatial models are replaced with element-wise multiplications, transforming local dynamics into spectral coordinate modifications.
What if we run the NCA entirely in the frequency domain? By updating the Fourier coefficients instead of spatial pixels, local update rules map to complex global modifications. We only need to store and update half of the spectrum due to conjugate symmetry, projecting back to space via the inverse FFT.

The Convolution Theorem & LTI NCA

Note: Standard spatial deep learning "convolutions" are often cross-correlations.
In the Fourier domain, spatial circular convolution maps to point-wise complex multiplication.

Let's revisit the convolution theorem. Linear Time-Invariant (LTI) cellular automata rely on convolution. In PyTorch, spatial convolutions are often implemented as cross-correlations and lack periodic boundaries. Operating in the FFT domain implements true circular convolution cleanly and efficiently.

Properties & Extended Horizons

Can Closed-Form Systems Regenerate?

  • Because they are linear systems, they lack the non-linear attractors typical of traditional NCAs.
  • However, they can be trained to map standing waves to target outputs.
  • When damaged, the underlying wave properties naturally regenerate the missing visual structures over time.

Non-Euclidean Horizons

  • Alternative Topologies: We can map coordinates onto manifolds like the Klein bottle using spectral coordinate adjustments.
  • Fractional FFT (FRFT): Offers a continuous transition between pure spatial and pure spectral domains, opening up intermediate coordinate spaces.
A common question: can these systems repair themselves? Since they are linear, they don't have non-linear attractors. However, by mapping standing waves to targets, physical wave mechanics naturally restore structural integrity after damage. We can also extend this work to complex topologies like Klein bottles or fractional Fourier spaces.

Summary & Resources

Integrating spectral methods into artificial life systems offers several useful capabilities:

  • Global context is handled naturally, avoiding the need for deep convolutional hierarchies.
  • Circular boundary conditions come for free, removing edge artifacts.
  • Closed-form equations allow us to study complex, long-term spatial trajectories analytically.
